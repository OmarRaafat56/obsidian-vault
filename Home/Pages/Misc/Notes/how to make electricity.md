[[Random facts]]

At its core, a generator is a machine that converts **mechanical energy** (movement) into **electrical energy**. It doesn’t actually "create" electricity; instead, it uses the movement of a conductor through a magnetic field to force electric charges to flow through an external circuit.
This process is based on **Electromagnetic Induction**, a principle discovered by Michael Faraday in 1831.
___
### 1. The Core Principle: Faraday’s Law
Faraday’s Law states that if you move a wire through a magnetic field, an electric current is "induced" in the wire. To make this happen, you need three main ingredients:
 1. **A Magnetic Field:** Usually provided by powerful permanent magnets or electromagnets.
 2. **A Conductor:** Usually a coil of copper wire.
 3. **Relative Motion:** Either the wire moves through the field, or the magnets spin around the wire.
 ___
### 2. How the Components Work Together
A standard generator consists of two primary parts:
 * **The Stator:** The stationary part of the generator. It usually contains the copper windings where the electricity is actually produced.
 * **The Rotor:** The rotating part. This is usually an electromagnet that spins inside the stator, powered by a mechanical source (like a turbine).
As the rotor spins, its magnetic field lines "cut" through the stationary copper wires of the stator. This creates a push-pull effect on the electrons inside the copper, causing them to move and creating a **voltage**.
___
### 3. Creating the Movement
Since a generator needs movement to work, it requires an external "prime mover" to spin the rotor. This is where different types of power plants come in:
 * **Hydroelectric:** Water falling through a dam spins the turbine.
 * **Wind:** The wind pushes the blades, which spin the internal shaft.
 * **Steam (Coal, Nuclear, or Gas):** Fuel is burned to boil water; the resulting high-pressure steam spins the turbine.
 * **Portable Generators:** An internal combustion engine (running on gasoline or propane) provides the mechanical force.
 ___
### 4. AC vs. DC
Most modern generators produce **Alternating Current (AC)**. Because the rotor is spinning, the magnetic poles passing the wires keep switching from North to South. This causes the electricity to change direction constantly—hence "alternating" current.
In a standard generator, the voltage (V) produced can be calculated using the formula:
$$V = N \cdot \frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t}$$
Where:
 * V is the induced voltage.
 * N is the number of turns in the wire coil.
 * \frac{\Delta \Phi}{\Delta t} is the rate of change of the magnetic flux (how fast the magnetic field is moving).
___
### Summary Table
| Component | Function |
|---|---|
| **Prime Mover** | The source of mechanical energy (engine, wind, water). |
| **Rotor** | The spinning magnet that creates a moving magnetic field. |
| **Stator** | The stationary copper coils that "collect" the induced current. |
| **Output** | Electrical energy sent to the power grid or your home. |
